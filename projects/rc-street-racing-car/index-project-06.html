From Concept to Curb: Designing and building a Bluetooth-Controlled RC Street Racing Car

In this semester-long project, I was part of a two-person team. We explored Bluetooth connectivity with a controller and receiver by creating an intuitive controller and a 3D printed street racing car.

Description

It was a challenging and rewarding project to work on. We were learning about the concepts at the same time we were designing and building it. There were many iterations for the controller and for building the car. However, it all started with the design. We knew that there were two integral parts for the whole idea to come together: the controller and the receiver. I was mainly in charge of getting all the components for both the controller and the car. Once this was done I was tasked to develop and 3D print the frame of the car and assemble it. In addition, because we only had one breadboard for the controller I created Jumper Connectors for the car. Creating and documenting all the connections that were needed for the car to function.

Components

Components for the controller
1 Arduino Nano 33 BLE board
1 single-color-LED bar graph
1 RGB LED
1 pressure pad (+ 1 capacitor)
1 button
1 Breadboard
Components for the car
1 Arduino Pro Mini (5V/16MHz) board
1 DC Motor
1 Hitec HS-53 servo
1 SparkFun Motor Driver - Dual TB6612FNG
1 DSD TECH HM-18 CC2640R2F Bluetooth 5.0 BLE Module
2 AA Battery Cases
1 9.0 voltage battery clip
1 3D Printer

The controller

We wanted the controller to be very intuitive for the user from the get-go. This is why we placed our Arduino Nano 33 BLE on a breadboard and we used the gyroscope information to determine the direction of the car, this way the user could be holding the breadboard as a wheel to control the car. The next thing we did was to use a pressure pad to simulate the acceleration from a pedal in a car, and used an LED bar graph as feedback so that the user could see how fast they were going by looking at how many lights on the bar graph were turned on, the more lights the faster the car was going. And finally we used a button and a single RGB LED light to indicate if the car was on or off.

The car & receiver

As for the car itself, we 3D printed all of the pieces and assembled them to accommodate the components. Because the Arduino Pro mini did not possess an inbuilt Bluetooth module such as the Arduino Nano 33 BLE board we had to connect an external module, this way information could be received from the controller. To manage the DC motor, for propulsion, and the servo for the direction we used a dual-motor driver. We used the AA battery cases to power the Arduino Pro Mini board and a 9.0 battery clip to power the servo and the DC motor.

Instructions

  1. Power on both the car and the controller. You should see the car's flashing light.
  2. When the car's LED stops blinking, and the controller's LED is green, you can start driving:
    1. To speed up, press on the pressure pad
    2. To turn left or right, tilt the controller left or right
    3. To stop, click the stop button. The controller's LED should be red
    4. To start, click the stop button again. The controller LED should be green
  3. If the controller stops showing lights, redo from step 1

What we learned

Building an idea is an iterative process. We started modifying the base 3D model in slicer files to fit in our motor and servo. We then assembled the Arduino Pro Mini, the TB6612FNG H-bridge Motor Driver, and the HM-18 Bluetooth module and later designed a flexible schematic to keep track of the wiring and update in case of needs. On the software side, we programmed and tested the logic of the boards that communicate via Bluetooth, the Arduino Pro Mini, and the Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense, which was challenging due to the required correct voltage, Bluetooth unreliability, and on-board pins limitation. Overall, two key challenges and takeaways are Bluetooth connection (reliable communication between a controller and a receiver) and hardware debugging (such as testing that all the wires pass electricity, proper grounding, etc.)
