Staying Hydrated on Campus: A UMass Hydration Station Locator App Prototype

In this semester-long project, I was part of a team that used a Human-Centered Design (HCD) framework to develop a mobile application prototype. The goal of the project was to create a user-friendly app that would help students and staff locate hydration stations on the UMass campus.

Design Framework Used

Human-Centered Design (HCD)

This is a design framework that develops solutions to problems by involving the human perspective in all steps of the problem-solving process.

The product must be self-explanatory. The user should be able to discover what actions are possible and how to perform them.

Users must be able to understand how the product is supposed to be used and what all the features mean.

Good HCD involves:

  • Observe - get the specifications of what will be designed
  • Define - specify what will be designed
  • Rapid Tests and Ideas - brainstorming
  • Iterate - best ideas are iterated and improved
  • Deliver - best possible solution is delivered, solving the user's needs

Introduction

1.1 Problem Statement

Our team has conducted a comprehensive analysis of the accessibility of hydration stations on the UMass campus and has identified a significant issue that requires immediate attention. Despite the presence of numerous hydration stations throughout the campus, students and visitors encounter considerable difficulty in locating them due to the lack of clear and readily accessible information. This deficiency poses a particular challenge when attempting to find hydration stations within unfamiliar buildings, consequently causing inconvenience, especially for individuals with limited time availability due to demanding academic schedules.

1.2 Previous Solutions

Currently, UMass provides a spreadsheet containing the locations of all hydration stations on campus; however, this approach fails to deliver a user-friendly experience and presents difficulties in locating and comprehending the necessary information. Consequently, students often resort to purchasing plastic bottled water, thereby incurring financial costs and contributing to the pervasive issue of single-use plastic waste, which poses significant environmental challenges.

1.3 Our Solution

In light of this predicament, our team is driven to resolve this problem by developing and implementing an innovative mobile application specifically designed to address the aforementioned issues. This application will offer students clear and user-friendly information concerning the exact locations of hydration stations across the UMass campus. By providing this essential resource, our solution aims to encourage and facilitate healthy hydration habits, diminish reliance on plastic bottled water, and actively contribute to the establishment of a more sustainable environment.

1.4 Motivation

Our endeavor is motivated by our unwavering commitment to enhancing the quality of life for students on campus and our ardent dedication to forging a more sustainable future. Our proposed solution aligns seamlessly with the ongoing initiatives at UMass, such as the UMass Zero Waste initiative and the Campus Race to Zero Waste 2023 competition. The Campus Race to Zero Waste competition stands as a national platform that empowers colleges and universities to advance their waste reduction efforts, encompassing vital aspects such as campus recycling and food waste diversion. By addressing the accessibility of hydration stations, our project aligns harmoniously with the overarching goals of these initiatives, amplifying their impact and propelling UMass toward greater sustainability and environmental stewardship.

Personas

In the early development of an app, stories and personas play a crucial role in building a user-centered product. These personas are fictional characters representing different user types likely to use the app. Personas help developers understand user needs, motivations, and behaviors.

For our first persona, we created Walt Jr. a highly active nursing student here at the University of Massachusetts Amherst. Walt goes to the gym, runs, and plays sports and therefore is always trying his best to stay hydrated. However, he always gets frustrated as he cannot find the hydration stations around campus. This prevents him from reaching his goals and he is always trying to find a better way to keep track or find new water stations around campus. This is why Walt Jr. is a great primary persona, as his goals and frustrations line up with what we wish to solve and our app will satisfy his needs and help him stay hydrated throughout the day.

For our second persona, we created Skylar White, an environmental science student here at the University of Massachusetts Amherst. While she is not as active as Walt Jr. she has other reasons for using our app to satisfy her goals. Skylar is frustrated at the rate at which global warming and other environmental factors are becoming more and more of an issue but since she is an active player on campus when it comes to environmental activism and awareness, she does her best to make her voice heard. Specifically, Skylar wants to support the UMASS Zero Waste Initiative by promoting the use of hydration stations around campus. Therefore, our app idea would be an excellent tool for her to get one step closer to her goals and promote the use of hydration stations by students here at UMASS.

Features

From task 1 (Walt Jr., Gym) we realized most of the buildings have multiple hydration stations. It would be a good idea to have a digital Hydration Station Map on our application. This map will show the locations of all the hydration stations at that particular building. This will help a person locate the nearest station easily, no matter where they are in the building.

From task 2 (Skylar White), we realized that our application could also include a search function that allows students to search for hydration stations based on location or other criteria. For example, they could search for stations near a particular building or those with a specific type of water filtration system.

From task 3 (Walt Jr., UMASS Track Team), we realized that our application could include a nearest hydration station functionality, this is a feature that automatically uses the person's current location to give the user a list of the top 10 nearest hydration stations based on their location. This goes well with our second feature from task 2, in case a user does not want the application to use their current location, then they could use the search functionality described in task 2.

Low-Fidelity Prototype

For our low-fidelity prototypes, we quickly drew what our app would look like on paper. We used this to get a general idea of what we wanted our app to look like and what features we wanted to include.

Mid-Fidelity Prototype

For our mid-fidelity prototype, we used Figma because it was easy to use and fitted our needs the most out of the other tools we could have chosen. Our prototype depicts the following tasks: a main page that the user sees when they open the app, a search function that will let users search for a specific building when they choose to use the app without location tracking, general settings related to the app, user settings to change the username, and an information page for users to see the specific water fountain they are looking for. We learned through this process that we could take out some things that we planned on earlier, but realized were not needed for the final product that we wanted to release.

High-Fidelity Prototype

We implemented a Map page, Search Page, and an Information page to help a user access different hydration stations across campus. With the Map Page, they can see where they are located as well as where each station is located in real-time. Furthermore, with the Search Page, a user can search for and find buildings that have hydration stations. Finally, the Information Page displays any and all relevant information for each building such as the number of hydration stations, the floors they are located on, and the water count for each station.
